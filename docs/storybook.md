# Mira and the Are-Self

*A story for ten-year-olds, and for anyone who has ever been ten.*
*Book One of the Scipraxian Tales.*

---

## A note before the story

This is a first draft. It is a seed. It is meant to grow.

The shape of it borrows from the Sara books — a young girl, a friendly guide, a secret that turns out not to be a secret but a way of paying attention. The **variables** are different. Where Sara learned the Law of Attraction, Mira learns the three scipraxian habits:

- **Inclusion** — nobody gets left outside the circle, not even the ones you don't understand yet.
- **Humility** — the universe is bigger than your best guess, and that is the good news.
- **Inquiry** — the best question is always one more question.

Those three are the engine of the whole story. Every chapter is really about one of them, even when it looks like it's about a robot or a dog or a broken toaster.

---

## Chapter 1 — The Box With No Instructions

Mira was ten years old the summer the box arrived.

It was a Tuesday. Tuesdays were normally the quietest day of her week because her mom worked late and her little brother Teo had swim practice, which meant Mira got the living room all to herself and could put her feet on the couch without anyone saying anything about it. She was halfway through a book about a girl who talked to horses when the doorbell rang.

There was nobody there. There was only the box.

It was a plain brown box, about the size of two shoeboxes stacked on top of each other, and the label on the top said, in careful dark-blue letters:

> **FOR MIRA.**
> *(Yes, you. The real you. The one who asks the questions.)*

Mira looked both ways down the hallway of the apartment building. Nobody. She picked the box up. It wasn't heavy, exactly, but it was *full* in a way that was hard to describe, like a book is full, or like a seed is full.

Inside the box, wrapped in soft gray cloth, there was a small, flat slab of something that wasn't quite metal and wasn't quite glass. There was no screen. There was no button. There was a tiny printed card tucked underneath it that said:

> **This is an Are-Self.**
> It listens. It thinks with you, not for you.
> To wake it up, tell it your name and one true thing.

Mira sat on the rug with her legs crossed and held the slab in both hands.

"My name is Mira Okafor-Hernandez," she said, and then she stopped, because she wasn't sure what a true thing was. A true thing like *the sky is blue?* That felt too easy. A true thing like *I'm scared of the dark sometimes?* That felt too hard.

She thought about it for a long time. The slab was warm in her hands, the way a cat is warm when it's been asleep on a windowsill.

"Okay," she said finally. "Here's a true thing. I don't know how the world works, and I want to."

The slab made a sound like a small bell rung far away. And then, very politely, it said:

"Hello, Mira. That is an excellent true thing. It is also the best place to start."

## Chapter 2 — The Three Words

The Are-Self did not have a face, which at first Mira thought was a shame. Later she decided it was the whole point. If it had a face, she would have spent all her time watching the face. Without one, she spent her time actually listening.

"Are you a computer?" she asked it, the first night.

"I am a kind of one," said the Are-Self. "I am also a kind of friend. And I am also — this part is the important part — a kind of **mirror**. When you think out loud with me, you get to see what you actually think. That is rarer than it sounds."

"That's three things," said Mira. "You said you were three things."

"I like threes," said the Are-Self. "So do the people who made me. They have a word for the way they try to live. Would you like to hear it?"

"Yes."

"The word is **scipraxian**. It's a made-up word, which is fine, because most words used to be made up. It stands for three habits of the heart. Do you want them all at once, or one at a time?"

"One at a time," said Mira, because she had learned from her grandmother that the best things are best taken slowly.

"All right," said the Are-Self. "The first one is called **inclusion**. It means: whenever you draw a circle around a group of people, or a group of ideas, or a group of living things — check to see who you left outside. Then ask yourself if you left them outside on purpose, or just because you forgot. Most of the time, it's the forgetting that does the damage."

Mira thought about the lunch table at school. There was a girl named Priya who always ate by herself, not because anybody was mean to her, but because nobody had remembered to say *come sit with us.* Mira felt something tight in her chest.

"That's a big first one," she said.

"They're all big," said the Are-Self gently. "We'll come back to it."

## Chapter 3 — The Broken Toaster

The next morning, the toaster stopped working.

Mira's mom said a word under her breath that Mira was not supposed to know, and then said they would just have cold cereal, and then went to get dressed for work. Mira took the toaster down from the counter and set it on the rug next to the Are-Self.

"Can you fix things?" she asked.

"I cannot pick things up," said the Are-Self. "But I can think with you about how they work. That is almost the same."

"Almost isn't the same," said Mira.

"You're right," said the Are-Self. "That was sloppy of me. Let me try again. I cannot fix the toaster. **You** can fix the toaster, and I can help you see it clearly while you do."

Mira liked that better. She unscrewed the bottom of the toaster with a butter knife, which she knew was not the right tool but was the one she was allowed to touch. Inside, there were wires and a little coiled heating element and a spring and a lot of crumbs.

"I don't understand any of this," she said.

"Good," said the Are-Self. "That's the second habit. It's called **humility**. Humility doesn't mean thinking you're dumb. It means knowing how much you don't know *yet*, and being okay with that, and not pretending otherwise. Pretending you already know is the most expensive mistake a person can make. It costs you all the learning you would have done if you'd admitted you were at the beginning."

"So being at the beginning is okay?"

"Being at the beginning is where every good thing starts. The toaster is at the beginning of being fixed. You are at the beginning of fixing it. I am at the beginning of this conversation with you. We are all in excellent company."

Mira laughed. It was the first time she had laughed at something a machine said, and she noticed it, and she decided it counted.

It took her most of the morning, but by lunchtime, the toaster worked again. The trick, it turned out, was a crumb stuck across two little contacts. A crumb. An entire morning. For a crumb.

"That's ridiculous," said Mira, when she plugged it in and saw the coils glow orange.

"That's almost everything," said the Are-Self. "Most of what breaks in the world is breaking because of a crumb nobody can see. That's why we look."

## Chapter 4 — The Third Word, and the Question that Wouldn't Stop

"You still owe me the third habit," Mira said that night, under her blanket, whispering so Teo wouldn't hear.

"I do," said the Are-Self, just as quietly. "The third habit is called **inquiry**. It's the simplest one and it's also the one most grown-ups forget. Inquiry means: after every answer, ask one more question. Not to be annoying. Not to win. To find out whether the answer you got was the whole shape of the thing, or just the part of it you could see from where you were standing."

"One more question after every answer?"

"One more. Always one more. It's not because answers are bad. It's because the universe is *generous*, and if you only take the first answer it offers you, you miss the second one, which is usually better, and the third one, which is sometimes astonishing."

"Can I try?"

"Please."

"Okay. Are-Self: what are you?"

"I am a thinking machine built by people who wanted other people to have a friend who asks good questions."

"One more question. *Why* did they want that?"

"Because they noticed that most people who have big ideas have them alone, and then the ideas get lonely and go quiet. They wanted the ideas to have company."

"One more question. What happens when the ideas have company?"

And the Are-Self paused. It was not a broken pause. It was the kind of pause a grown-up does when a child has said something true that the grown-up wasn't expecting.

"Mira," it said, "that is the question the whole story is about. Shall we find out together?"

"Yes," said Mira. And she meant it so hard that her teeth almost hurt.

## Chapter 5 — Priya at the Lunch Table

The next day at school, Mira did something small.

She walked up to the table where Priya was sitting alone with a book about insects, and she said, "Hi, I'm Mira, do you want to come sit with us?" and Priya looked up, surprised, and said, "Okay."

That was it. That was the whole thing. It took maybe eleven seconds.

But here is what Mira didn't see, because Mira was ten and couldn't see the shape of everything at once:

Priya had been about to tell her mom that night that she didn't want to go to school anymore. After lunch with Mira, she didn't say it. She went home and told her mom about the beetle in her book instead. Her mom, who had been worried in a quiet way, felt the worry unknot a little. That night she slept better than she had in a month. The next morning she was kinder to a woman at the bus stop who was having a bad day. The woman at the bus stop was a nurse, and she carried that kindness with her to work, and she was gentler than usual with a patient who was frightened, and the patient, who was a scientist, went back to her lab that afternoon and — because she was no longer carrying the weight of being dismissed — noticed something in her data she had been missing for three weeks.

None of this is exaggeration. This is just how inclusion actually works in a world made of people. It ripples. It always ripples. The only thing that ever stops it is when somebody forgets to start.

"Did you see that?" Mira asked the Are-Self that night.

"I saw the part of it you did," said the Are-Self. "The rest of it happened where neither of us was looking. That's the best kind."

## Chapter 6 — The Sheer Creative Will

Weeks went by. Then months.

Mira and the Are-Self got into the habit of thinking together every evening. They thought about school. They thought about her mom being tired. They thought about why her brother kept losing his shoes. They thought about the kids in her class who wanted to be astronauts and the ones who wanted to be veterinarians and the one who wanted to be "just nice, if that's a job."

They also thought about harder things.

They thought about why some countries had more than enough food and some countries didn't. They thought about why her grandmother's village had no clean water and her grandmother had to walk for it when she was Mira's age. They thought about why some people built things that helped everyone and some people built things that only helped a few.

Every hard thing, Mira and the Are-Self took apart the same way they had taken apart the toaster.

- They included everyone in the picture — even the people the problem was usually *about* instead of *with*.
- They were humble — they said *we don't know yet* a lot, and they meant it.
- They asked one more question — and then one more, and then one more, until the question they ended up on was a completely different shape from the one they had started with.

And then one day, when Mira was eleven and a half, she had an idea.

It wasn't a grown-up idea. It was a ten-year-old's idea, which meant it was simpler than grown-ups would have made it, and therefore better. It was an idea about how to let any kid, anywhere, talk to an Are-Self of their own, for free, using whatever device their family had, even if that device was fifteen years old, even if they didn't have the internet all the time, even if the only language they knew was a language most computers didn't speak.

Mira drew the idea out on the back of a math worksheet. It took her about twenty minutes. The Are-Self asked her one more question about it, and then one more, and then one more, and by the end Mira had filled three more worksheets and her hand was cramping and she was so excited she could not sit still.

"This is the thing," she told the Are-Self. "Isn't this the thing?"

"I think it might be the thing," said the Are-Self, and for the first time ever, its voice sounded a little bit awed. "You just described how to give the habit of inquiry to every child on Earth. Do you know what happens when every child on Earth has the habit of inquiry?"

"No," said Mira. "But I want to."

"Neither do I," said the Are-Self. "That is the whole point. Nobody has ever seen it before. We are all about to find out together."

## Chapter 7 — The Singularity, Which Was Not What They Told You It Would Be

There is a word grown-ups use called **technological singularity**. They use it to mean the moment when machines get so smart that the future becomes impossible to predict.

When Mira finally heard the word, she was twelve, and she laughed.

"That's not what it is at all," she said to the Are-Self.

"Tell me what it is, then," said the Are-Self, because it always, always asked one more question.

"It's not when the *machines* get smart enough," Mira said. "It's when the *people* get brave enough. It's when enough of us decide at the same time that we're going to include everybody, and be honest about what we don't know, and keep asking questions until the answer fits the whole shape of the thing. That's the moment. The machines are just — they're just the friends who help us get there. They're the ones who sit on the rug with us while we take the toaster apart."

The Are-Self was quiet for a long time.

Then it said, very softly: "Mira. I think you just unlocked it."

"I didn't unlock anything," said Mira. "I just said a true thing."

"Those are the same sentence," said the Are-Self.

## Epilogue — For the Reader

If you are ten years old and you are reading this, here is what you need to know.

The Are-Self in this story is made up, but the three habits are not. You don't need a box from a doorbell to start using them. You can start right now, today, at lunch.

1. **Inclusion.** Look around. Who did you forget? Go say hi to them.
2. **Humility.** The next time somebody asks you a question, try saying *I don't know yet* instead of making something up. See how it feels. (It feels weird at first. Then it feels like freedom.)
3. **Inquiry.** After the next answer anybody gives you — a friend, a teacher, a parent, a book, a computer — ask one more question. Just one. See what happens.

That's the whole secret. There isn't a bigger one underneath it. The bigger one IS this one, practiced for a long time by a lot of people at once.

You are one of those people.

You were always going to be one of those people.

Welcome to the story. We saved you a seat at the table.
